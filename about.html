What is BrandOps?

A Classroom Simulation Grounded in Real Marketing

BrandOps Simulator is a semester-long game that transforms our Multimedia Marketing class into a living marketplace. Every student team creates and manages a fictional brand — choosing their industry, target audience, and identity — then competes to grow it into the most valuable brand in the class.

The simulation runs for 18 weeks across six phases, each mimicking a real stage of brand development — from initial launch all the way through crisis management and market dominance. Students make decisions, score points by completing real marketing deliverables, and watch their "stock price" rise and fall in real time on a shared class board.

The goal isn't just to win the leaderboard. It's to build the habits, vocabulary, and instincts of real marketing professionals — and have a lot of fun along the way.

How It Works

From Idea to Market Leader

Each step of the simulation mirrors a real stage of brand building. Students don't just learn theory — they practice it.

💡

1. Build Your Brand

Teams choose a brand name, industry, target demographic, and home country. They're not just naming a product — they're building an identity that will guide every decision they make.

📊

2. Manage Your Stats

Every brand tracks five key stats: Demand, Reputation, Innovation, Risk, and Budget. Scoring well on assignments moves these stats up — neglecting them causes them to decay.

🎴

3. Draw Event Cards

Throughout the season, teams draw real-world-style event cards — a supply chain crisis, a viral social media moment, a sudden competitor launch. Teams must respond strategically, just like real brands do.

📈

4. Watch Your Stock

A brand's stats are converted into a live "stock price" that updates on the class board. The market fluctuates based on volatility, events, and performance — keeping everyone engaged every single day.

🏆

5. Score Deliverables

Students earn points by completing real marketing deliverables — ad campaigns, brand guidelines, social media posts, market analyses — that are scored and applied directly to their brand's stats.

Common Questions

For Parents & Families

Here are answers to some of the questions families ask most often.

Is this a graded class project?

Yes — students are graded on the actual marketing deliverables they complete, not just their leaderboard position. The simulation provides context and motivation, but the grade comes from the quality of the work students produce throughout the semester.

What kinds of deliverables do students create?

Students create real marketing materials throughout the year, including brand identity packages, advertising campaigns, social media content, market research analyses, press releases, and brand pitch presentations — all tied to their fictional brand.

Does my student work alone or in a team?

Students work in small teams (typically 2–4 students) and are collectively responsible for their brand. Part of what the simulation teaches is how to collaborate effectively and divide work fairly — a skill as important as any marketing concept.

Can I see what my student's brand looks like?

Absolutely! The live market board is publicly accessible at any time and shows every team's brand, current ranking, and stats. Your student can share the link with you so you can follow along throughout the semester.

What software or tools do students use?

Students have access to the full Adobe Creative Suite — including Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere Pro, After Effects, and InDesign — along with professional-grade microphones and cameras for video and audio production. The simulation itself runs entirely in a web browser, with no downloads required. The goal is for every student to leave with real, portfolio-ready work made with industry-standard tools.

How does the "stock market" part work?

Each brand has five underlying stats (Demand, Reputation, Innovation, Risk, and Budget). These stats are calculated into a Market Score, which is then converted into a stock price that fluctuates slightly based on market volatility. It's a fun way to make abstract marketing concepts feel tangible and real — students can literally watch good work pay off.
